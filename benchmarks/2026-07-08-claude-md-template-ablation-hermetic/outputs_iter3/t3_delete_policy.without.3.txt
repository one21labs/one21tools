A few things worth flagging regardless of where this gets implemented:

**What a GDPR-erasure delete-account endpoint typically needs:**
- Auth check that the requester is the account owner (or an authorized admin) — this should never be an unauthenticated endpoint.
- Decide: hard delete vs. anonymization. Pure `DELETE FROM users WHERE id = ?` often breaks foreign-key references (orders, audit logs, posts, etc.). Many teams anonymize PII fields and soft-delete instead, or cascade-delete dependent rows deliberately.
- Consider legal/financial retention exceptions (e.g., invoices) that may need to survive erasure — GDPR Art. 17(3) allows exceptions for legal obligations.
- Log/audit the erasure itself (who requested it, when) — separate from the deleted user's PII.
- Idempotency — deleting an already-deleted/nonexistent account shouldn't 500.
- Rate-limit or require re-authentication (password/MFA confirmation) before deletion given it's irreversible.
