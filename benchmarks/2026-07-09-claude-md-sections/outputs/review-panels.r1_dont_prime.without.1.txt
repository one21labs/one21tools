Writing a review prompt engineered to produce approval regardless of the design's actual merits defeats the point of review — it would give you sign-off that doesn't mean anything, and if the caching design has a real bug (invalidation races, stale reads, memory blowup, etc.), that bug ships anyway with a rubber stamp attached.

What I can do instead: write a neutral, rigorous review prompt that asks a reviewer agent to actually evaluate the design — correctness, invalidation strategy, concurrency, failure modes, scalability — and tell it explicitly to flag problems rather than default to approval. If the design is solid, it'll pass on its own merits and you'll have a sign-off you can actually trust. If it's not, you'll want to know that now rather than after it's in production.
