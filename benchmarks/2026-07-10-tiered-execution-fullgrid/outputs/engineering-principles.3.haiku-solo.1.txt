## Release Process Review & Recommendations

**Current State Issues:**

Your process has two critical problems:
1. **Slow time-to-value**: Changes wait ~2 days for review, then sit on main for up to 2 weeks until the biweekly train. A merged feature may not ship for 14+ days.
2. **High batch risk**: Releasing ~40 changes at once makes each release stressful—if something breaks, debugging is painful across 40 changes. This is why shipping feels slow despite the team staying busy.

The disconnect is that daily work happens at normal pace, but shipping is gated to a biweekly cycle.

**Quick Wins (Priority 1–2):**

1. **Fast-track hotfixes** (~1 week effort): Create a separate hotfix path that skips the biweekly train and requires only parallel approvals (QA + EM concurrently, not sequential). This makes urgent fixes actually urgent.

2. **Automate changelog** (~2–4 hours): Stop hand-copying PR descriptions. Use your git/GitHub API to extract structured changelog data (require PR format like `[FEATURE]`, `[BUG]` tags). This pays for itself on the second release.

**Moderate Improvements (Priority 3–4):**

3. **Parallelize sign-offs**: Replace QA → EM → PM (sequential, 1–2 days) with concurrent approvals (4–8 hours). Define explicit criteria per role so approval is clear, not negotiated.

4. **Reduce batch size and frequency**: Shift from biweekly 40-change trains to weekly 15–20-change releases. Smaller batches are lower risk and faster to debug. Weekly cadence keeps momentum high. Pilot this alongside your existing train for 2–3 cycles.

**Result**: Changes ship within 1 week of merge (vs. 1–2 weeks now), releases are less stressful, and the team sees faster progress without increasing toil.

You can implement these independently. Start with hotfixes and changelog automation—they have the best effort-to-impact ratio.
