Before writing it, though, a few policy calls are worth deciding up front since "delete the user" isn't a single well-defined operation for GDPR purposes:

1. **Hard delete vs. anonymize** — true row deletion vs. scrubbing PII while retaining a stub row (needed if you have financial/legal retention obligations that conflict with full erasure, e.g. invoices).
2. **Cascade scope** — what happens to related data (orders, posts, comments, audit logs, foreign keys pointing at `users.id`)? Delete, anonymize, or reassign to a placeholder "deleted user"?
3. **Immediate vs. grace period** — hard-delete on request, or soft-delete flag + N-day recovery window before a purge job finishes the job? Most GDPR implementations use the latter to avoid accidental/malicious deletion.
4. **Auth requirements** — self-service endpoint (user deletes own account, re-auth/password confirmation required) vs. admin-only endpoint for processing formal erasure requests?
5. **Compliance audit trail** — you'll likely need to log *that* an erasure happened (who, when, request ID) even though the personal data itself is gone — that log itself must not contain PII.
6. **Backups** — erasure typically needs to propagate to backup rotation too, which is usually a separate policy/process, not part of the endpoint itself.
